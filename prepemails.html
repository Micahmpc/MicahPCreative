A Rules of Engagement

How does one improve the effectiveness of communication as a brand?

During the start of this project, mobile engagement for email was a year or so into its eclipse of desktop. Meaning, the majority of emails were now being read (or not read, for that matter) on mobile devices. Prep Sportswear was starting to focus on an internal paradigm shift to slow down on resources for product innovation and make sure our back-end was up to snuff and keeping pace with the times. At the time, email was lagging behind, and we didn't have the developer resources to put anyone from the dev team on the task of building them. Email is also a beast of its own nature, as the semantic structure is very limited, the styling all has to be done internally or inline, and, depending on which mail client the user is on, may or may not support certain elements of code.

So, I begged and pleaded my creative director to send me to an email development class so I could take matters into my own hands. There were actually 2 similar email workshops offered at the School of Visual Concepts, so we decided to both take one, mine on the development end, and hers on the content and marketing end. The entire email program for Prep at the time was basically placed in our laps after the majority of the email marketing department no longer existed, and the Creative team (under the Marketing team umbrella) had absorbed the responsibility. The class would actually serve as my first real step toward learning front-end development.

As mentioned before, the paradigm had shifted for email to mobile users being the biggest group. The emails we had been using were 90% desktop only, meaning on 90% of our emails when the user opened them on their mobile device, the same content that one would see on their computer was shrunk to a fraction of the size rendering it virtually impossible to read. The branding was another thing I wanted to address, after noticing that our marketing emails had a different branded look than our triggered/transactional emails, and sometimes weren't even consistent with other marketing emails. I went with my director through all the current emails and templates we actively used, and came up with a few key points we needed to address.

  • Optimize for responsive layouts, and viewing across device types
  • Ensure proper functionality across ALL email clients.
  • Improve branding across all emails to create cohesion.
  • Create a set of marketing templates that cater to different content and targeting strategies
  • Make emails more engaging for customers
  • Dynamic tile technology

The Bullet Points

Optimize for cross-device functionality

This one was pretty straightforward. Build the emails in a such a way that they can be viewed on any device, and will look good, and correct. This alone likely was the largest contributor to success in this project. Seems logical enough, but with other more pressing company initiatives going on, things like "building emails for mobile" can fall low on the list of must-haves. The effect of email on sales though is another story. More on this later.

Ensure proper functionality across ALL email clients

The average person probably never thinks about the difference between Gmail, Yahoo Mail, and Outlook (among others). Web browsers, for example, vary slightly in their handling of certain elements of code, but overall can be accounted for by using conventions like '-webkit'. Email on the other hand never saw the benefits of much standardization. Each client with a different set of quirks or rules that they decided would or would not work. This makes styling email templates a total pain in the butt.

Before releasing a template, it had to go through several rounds of testing. First me as the developer, then passed out to another designer, our creative director, and our email marketing manager. The three of us forming the Musketeers of Prep Emails. The point here being that we can't assume a user is going to play by the rules of using the more popular email clients only, as many of our users were adults/parents ages 35-55 (while they may have been tech-savvy enough for the latest and greatest, soccer camp can take priority over a new version of Microsoft Outlook.). We did end up having to compromise on a few outlier versions like Outlook 2008, but it was well into the 20-teens, so too bad. The monetary worth of those customers was not enough to outway other sacrifices that would accommodate.

Creating Cohesion through branding

Prep's strategy relied heavily on the amount of content and included users. Which meant much of the focus was on scoring new customers but lacked in the area of retention. One of the contributors here in my mind was email. Prior, we had a bunch of emails that didn't look alike and didn't sell our product very well. They were largely text-based with a few static images of potential products to buy, but not necessarily catering to the individual. The result? Very low click-through and conversion rates, high unsubscribe rates, and occasionally being filtered to spam.

One of the main focuses throughout the design of all the templates was making sure that the static elements had a branded look that created familiarity with Prep Sportswear. Even down to the details, we decided we even needed to standardize our email titles, so we begin the building/interactions of our relationship with the customer before they ever open the emails. That familiarity would hopefully result in higher open and click-through rates.

Building specialized templates and increasing engagement

We decided the perfect number of templates for the marketing emails was 3. The first was product-centric. The point was to highlight a handful of products (usually linked to a current promotion) that the user could click-through right to the design page of the website (the final step before cart). This was really meant as our "product-dump" template that let our user essentially shop within the email for a number of generic high-selling items and designs.

The second template was geared more toward a generic company email that used high-definition imagery in big bold blocks, that drove customers into the website with dazzling backgrounds and a glimpse into their future if they wore our product. This of course was inspired by many of the large players in the recreation/sports spaces like Adidas, The Clymb, and Fanatics.

The last template, specifically for but not exclusive to our college market, featured a branded email that showed off a full head-to-toe product arrangement, and a branded scheme, pulled from our data on their favorite or most-recently searched stores. It essentially served as window shopping for our customers, the kind with mannequins on display in a full outfit, where you can buy a piece or the whole thing. It also gave the user that college bookstore feel that Prep stressed in its business model.

The purpose of all of these was to make the email simpler for the user to interpret what we wanted/wished for them to do, which is come on in and buy stuff. Creating the specialized versions allowed us to better choose the interaction we wanted based on the purpose of the email. The first being to show the products we featured, the second to drive users to the site, and the third to speak directly to them and create an experience tailored specifically to them (Hey dude, I made this just for you. Feel free to look around.)

The fun part - NEW TECHNOLOGY!

I absolutely love trying to create new technology. So the latter 2 templates were built forward-thinking, with a new approach that was not yet developed. We had created events to work with Responsys and our website, that would take the individual customers preferences and info (favorite stores, name, etc.), and allow us to provide nothing but a URL with dynamic selectors to give each customer their own custom email (out of batches in the 10's of thousands). In other words, we put a URL we had created into Responsys, and when the user got it, that link - usually an image - would show a product from their favorite store. This was all fine and good. But not enough for me.

Within the URL, there was a legacy piece that didn't work really regarding scale. In fact, you could omit it completely and it wouldn't change anything. The problem though was that it only showed the full image, which was a product shot from the website, of a product in full, at a 3/4 angle. To make the designs really pop and look up to snuff with the new athletic standard of cool imagery in their content, we needed to be able to crop the images to fit better with the surrounding content and not just floating around on top of it.

I worked out a minimum-viable-product with our wiz software engineer (a former Navy Seal engineer, turned software engineer). I decided on how we wanted to administer the crop function, within the bounds of the URL. There needed to be two dimensions, vertical and horizontal, and a third dimension for scale. The vertical would be administered as a percentage of height from zero (top of the product shot referenced), the horizontal as a percentage from the middle out, and the scale, which would zoom in or out accordingly. Then my spy friend went into our repository and made those things happen. A URL based crop and zoom, simple enough that any new employee or designer working on emails could edit a product shot on the fly, functionally removing the designer from this part of the equation, and putting it rightfully into the hands of the email team to customize the templates without needing our help.

The Aftermath

Numbers sky-rocketed. Before releasing the whole set and figuring out our new technology we were building, we release the first email template. Open rates went up due to lack of Spam filtering. Click-through rates skyrocketed from their previous average, and conversion rates crept up, and bounce rates dropped. Most of this is simply due to the most basic improvement of making them responsive. But all the numbers held up with future sends and were back in line with industry averages or higher, and prompted the redoing of the triggered emails as well. A project I would start before handing off, as I moved on to build the front-end of the new website. The technology we had built, we were also able to apply to the platform of both the current website and the one we were beginning to build.

The other half of this project that goes fairly unseen actually, is that what it really also gave us was a better process through standardization. The design team no longer had to constantly update email code, or create assets on assets for a single email. When they did, they had a style guide to work of off to answer any questions. And everything was put in the hands of the email team, with the exception of custom hero image creation. It ended up saving all teams involved a lot of headaches, took out a lot of the need for communication and requests between busy teams, and, well, they looked a lot cooler.
